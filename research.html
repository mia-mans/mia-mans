THE MILKY WAY SYSTEM
& LARGE MAGELLANIC CLOUD

All galaxies have satellite galaxies that orbit, fall into, and otherwise interact with their host galaxy. Our Milky Way galaxy has one satellite of note: the Large Magellanic Cloud, or LMC. The LMC is a massive satellite estimated to be roughly 10-20% of the Milky Way’s mass. As a result, its interactions with the Milky Way have a significant impact on the kinematic structure of the Milky Way.

The dynamic features of Milky Way-LMC interactions can be separated into a local and a global effect on the Milky Way. While the expected effects are well understood, the existing observational data is not dense enough to accurately identify any perturbations. However, emerging asteroseismic methods provide a promising solution. Asteroseismic distances from M giant stars will provide an unprecedented look into the outer regions of the Milky Way and the effects of the LMC.

In order to prepare for upcoming asteroseismic catalogs, I’ve worked with Dr. Robyn Sanderson, Dr. Daniel Huber, and Daniel Hey to identify signatures of massive satellite interactions in FIRE-2 cosmological simulations and predict their observability in mock stellar surveys using py-ananke. We are able to isolate a strong local signature in the simulation host galaxy, and are working to identify the signal in RR Lyrae and M giant mock surveys.

This project began through the REU program at the University of Hawaii Institute for Astronomy. See my 2023 talk and 2023 AAS iPoster on this work.
